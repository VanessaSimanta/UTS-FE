Do You Know? Noncommunicable Diseases Kill 41 Million People Each Year Because of Poor Lifestyle

Noncommunicable diseases (NCDs), also known as chronic diseases, are the result of a combination of genetic, physiological, environmental, and behavioral factors. The main types of NCDs are cardiovascular diseases (such as heart attacks and stroke), cancers, chronic respiratory diseases (such as chronic obstructive pulmonary disease and asthma), and diabetes.

According to WHO, NCDs are mostly caused by modifiable behavioral risk factors such as unhealthy diet, tobacco use, physical inactivity, harmful use of alcohol, and metabolic risk factors such as raised blood pressure, overweight/obesity, hyperglycemia, hyperlipidemia, and environmental risk factors such as air pollution.


Facts About Heart Attacks

  • Cardiovascular diseases are the leading cause of death worldwide, as highlighted by a survey from Our World in Data.
  • This type of disease is a major risk factor for heart attacks, which are often fatal.
  • According to data from the Global Burden of Disease and the Institute for Health Metrics and Evaluation (IHME) from 2014 to 2019, heart disease remains the top cause of death in Indonesia, responsible for 14.38% of all fatalities.
  • The Basic Health Research (Riskesdas) reports a concerning trend, with the prevalence of heart disease rising significantly from 0.5% in 2013 to 1.5% in 2018.
  • Similarly, in the United States, heart disease is also the leading cause of death, with someone experiencing a heart attack every 40 seconds.

What Is Diabetes?

According to WHO (World Health Organization), diabetes is a chronic disease that occurs either when the pancreas does not produce enough insulin or when the body cannot effectively use the insulin it produces. Insulin is a hormone that regulates blood glucose. Hyperglycemia, also called raised blood glucose or raised blood sugar, is a common effect of uncontrolled diabetes and over time leads to serious damage to many of the body's systems, especially the nerves and blood vessels.

In 2019, diabetes and kidney disease due to diabetes caused an estimated 2 million deaths. According to the International Diabetes Federation (IDF), Indonesia ranks fifth among countries with the highest number of diabetes cases, with 19.5 million sufferers in 2021, and is projected to reach 28.6 million by 2045.


Did You Know There Are Two Types of Diabetes?

  • Type 1 Diabetes

    Type 1 diabetes is characterized by deficient insulin production and requires daily administration of insulin.

  • Type 2 Diabetes

    Type 2 diabetes affects how your body uses sugar (glucose) for energy. It stops the body from using insulin properly, which can lead to high levels of blood sugar if not treated.

According to the Indonesian Ministry of Health Regulation No. 30 of 2013, the recommended daily intake of fat should not exceed 67 grams per day, which is equivalent to 20-25% of your daily caloric intake. Sodium intake should also be limited to no more than 2,000 milligrams per day. At first glance, it might seem that eating KFC is acceptable since these numbers are below the recommended limits. Second, we need to be physically active for 30 minutes most days of the week

Exercise is essential for our bodies, but it doesn't always have to mean intense workouts or lifting heavy weights at the gym. Simple activities like taking a walk in the park, dancing to your favorite music, practicing yoga, or even jumping around playfully can be just as effective. By staying active, we can ward off various health risks such as obesity, diabetes, stroke, and high blood pressure. Beyond that, exercise also works wonders for our mental well-being, improving our mood, enhancing sleep quality, and giving us an extra boost of energy to get through the day.
Remember, it's only 30 minutes a day! Too much exercise isn't good for your body, so don't forget to track your time and drink plenty of water during and after your exercise.

Third, don't forget to get enough sleep, control your stress, and don't smoke

Why do we need sleep?

The Sleep Health Foundation recommends 9 to 11 hours of sleep for school-age children, 8 to 10 hours for teens, 7 to 9 hours for adults aged 18-64, and 7 to 8 hours for older adults (65 and over). Not getting enough sleep can lead to sleep deprivation, which can negatively impact your mood, energy, and mental health. Long-term risks of sleep deprivation include high blood pressure, high cholesterol, high blood sugar levels, diabetes, and Alzheimer's disease. If you don't get enough sleep, it will also affect your diet and physical activity.

What if we're having a bad day and feeling stressed?

Stress affects both the mind and the body. A little bit of stress is normal and can help us perform daily activities. However, too much stress can lead to physical and mental health issues. Learning how to manage stress is crucial for maintaining mental and physical well-being, so always prioritize your mental health!

Smoking?

We all know that smoking is bad for your health. Smoking can cause lung disease by damaging your airways and the small air sacs (alveoli) in your lungs. It also increases the risk of developing cancer in almost any part of your body, as well as type 2 diabetes. The risk of developing diabetes is 30-40% higher for active smokers than for non-smokers. So, it's best to quit smoking, as the health risks are too many to ignore.
